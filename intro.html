Introduction

EB Garamond is a classic serif typeface that works really well for body text, especially in books, articles, and anything that needs to feel timeless and readable. It has soft curves, balanced proportions, and a warm, traditional look that makes long reading feel smooth and easy.

The typeface was designed by Georg Duffner and later expanded by Octavio Pardo. It’s based on the work of Claude Garamond, a French type designer born in Paris around 1499 who died there in 1561. Garamond apprenticed under Antoine Augerau and worked with Geoffroy Tory before becoming one of the first independent punchcutters. His romans and the famous Grecs du Roi were cut for Robert Estienne, commissioned by King François I. Although he later tried publishing himself, he died in poverty despite his immense influence. EB Garamond is a digital revival of these old letterforms, using a 1592 specimen printed by Conrad Berner as the main reference.

EB Garamond is an old-style serif, part of the Garalde group. This category, named after Garamond and Aldus Manutius, represents typefaces from the late 15th to early 17th centuries. It includes classics such as Bembo, Garamond, Granjon, and later revivals like Sabon. Garalde typefaces share diagonal stress, moderate contrast between thick and thin strokes, and bracketed serifs that give them a hand-drawn feel. The lowercase letters have large counters and a nice rhythm, which helps with readability. Georg Duffner is best known for initiating EB Garamond in 2011, focusing on creating a high-quality, open-source Garamond family for Google Fonts, with plans for expansions into polytonic Greek, IPA, and ornaments. Octavio Pardo joined the project in 2017 to expand and refine it, ensuring broader weights, italics, and variable font formats for modern use. While EB Garamond is their most recognized work, their dedication to reviving and evolving Garamond’s legacy is itself a major contribution to contemporary typography.
